Research

My research focuses on worker mobility, compensation, and labor market dynamics. I combine individual-level data with reduced-form empirical methods to inform richer models of worker and firm behavior.

My job market paper studies the long term consequences of receiving a promotion. I find that asymmetric information about worker ability can fully explain observed wage dynamics for promoted and non-promoted workers. I use this setting to demonstrate how unemployment policy can affect worker sorting and wage dynamics. In ongoing work with Martin Gervais, we combine scraped data from Form 5500 filings with matched employer-employee data to study how retirement plan generosity affects worker mobility and compensation.

Personal

I was born in Ontario, Canada, but spent most of my childhood in Georgia, where I attended grade school. I completed my undergraduate degree at the University of Georgia and then continued directly into the PhD program there.

As a UGA student for nearly a decade, I have developed quite the fondness for Georgia athletics. I keep up with recruiting news and the NFL draft process, and try to watch every game each season. Outside of UGA sports, I am a lifelong Boston Red Sox fan and still have not fully recovered from losing Mookie Betts to the Dodgers. In my free time, I am an avid gym-goer and enjoy trying new local gyms when I travel. So far, my favorite is a small reservation gym in Kapuskasing, a small town in northern Ontario. During the winter, I enjoy skiing with my family and friends back in Canada.
