Research

My research statement can begin with a broad description of the economic questions that motivate my work, the literatures I contribute to, and the empirical or theoretical tools I rely on. This is a good place to explain how my current projects fit together and why those questions matter for labor markets, macroeconomics, or public policy.

A second paragraph can summarize the agenda I want to pursue over the next several years. For example, I may want to explain how my job market paper connects to future work on worker mobility, firm behavior, information frictions, or policy design, and how these projects build a coherent research portfolio.

Personal

My personal statement can include a short account of my background, how I became interested in economics, and the experiences that shaped my academic path. This section can be more conversational than the research statement and can help readers understand the perspective I bring to my work.

I may also want to add a few lines about interests outside of research, teaching, or campus life. This is a useful place for personal details that make the page feel more human while still staying professional and concise.
